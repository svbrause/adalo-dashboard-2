⭐ A Tactful, Professional Script You Can Use

Start by anchoring in their goals

“Since you mentioned you want to look your best and feel refreshed, I took a close look at the overall balance of your lower face.”

This tells them you are responding to their request, not pointing out flaws.

⸻

Normalize what you’re seeing

“One thing I’m noticing is that your masseter muscles — the chewing muscles along the sides of your jaw — are a bit stronger or fuller. This is really common and can happen from things like clenching, grinding, or even genetics.”

Patients relax when they hear: common, normal, not a defect.

⸻

Explain the aesthetic impact neutrally

“When these muscles get overactive, they can make the jawline look a little wider or more square, even if the rest of the face is delicate or slim.”

Use words like “shape,” “balance,” “proportion,” “definition,” instead of:
❌ big
❌ bulky
❌ masculine
❌ harsh

⸻

Offer options, not prescriptions

“A lot of patients who want a softer or more contoured jawline choose to relax this muscle with a small amount of neuromodulator. It doesn’t affect chewing—it just reduces some of the overactivity over time.”

Follow with two options so they don’t feel forced:
• “We can treat it very subtly if you want a gentle slimming effect.”
• “Or if you prefer a more defined angle, we can leave it as is. Both are valid—it really depends on the look you’re going for.”

⸻

Invite them in — never push

“Would you like me to show you what I’m seeing so you can decide if this is something you want to address?”

This keeps autonomy with the patient.
