Yes. The opportunity is **not just “AI skincare recommendations”** and not just “find a med spa near me.” The gap is the bridge between them:

> **A consumer iOS app that helps users understand their skin, track what is and isn’t working, then match them to trusted local aesthetic providers when at-home skincare is not enough.**

That is a much more differentiated wedge than a standalone AI skincare app or a generic provider directory.

## The core insight

Today, users discover aesthetic providers through fragmented, low-context channels: Google Maps, Yelp, RealSelf, Instagram, TikTok, Groupon, provider websites, referrals, and influencer content. Those channels are useful, but they usually do **not** know the user’s skin history, product routine, sensitivity, goals, budget, downtime tolerance, or treatment readiness.

There are some closer competitors. **RealSelf** has reviews, treatment education, before/afters, and provider discovery. **AEDIT** offers cosmetic procedure education, AI/3D try-on, and provider connections. **Upkeep** is a consumer marketplace for booking vetted aesthetic providers. **Mindbody, Booksy, Fresha, and Vagaro** provide broader beauty/wellness booking marketplaces. **Cherry** and Allē/Cherry focus more on financing and provider discovery. But the market still lacks a strong consumer-first app that starts with **skin intelligence and longitudinal tracking**, then converts that into **personalized local provider matching**. ([RealSelf.com][1])

The med spa/aesthetics supply side is also large and fragmented. AmSpa says the U.S. medical aesthetics industry has surpassed **$17B+ in revenue** and is growing by more than **$1B per year**, and its 2024 executive recap said U.S. medical spa locations grew from **8,899 in 2022 to 10,488 in 2023**. ([American Med Spa Association][2])

So yes: **lead generation for local aesthetic providers can be a strong business model**, but only if the consumer product earns trust first.

People who have tried products but still struggle with acne, texture, redness, pigmentation, dullness, pores, scarring, or sensitivity.

Best features for them: routine analysis, trial tracking, treatment readiness score, provider match.

People interested in Botox, facials, chemical peels, microneedling, lasers, IPL, HydraFacial, or fillers but overwhelmed by options.

Their problem:

Best features for them: education, provider vetting, realistic expectations, budget/downtime filters.

People already searching “Botox near me,” “best med spa for acne scars,” “microneedling near me,” “laser for dark spots,” etc.

“Google gives me 40 options and I can’t tell who is right for me.”

Their problem:
“I need someone who understands my skin, not just someone nearby.”

Best features for them: Fitzpatrick-aware education, hyperpigmentation risk prompts, provider specialty tags, inclusive before/after galleries.

# App concept

The app should feel less like Yelp and more like a **personal skin concierge**.

## Core user journey

### 1. Onboarding

The app asks:

- Skin goals: acne, texture, pores, redness, pigmentation, scarring, aging, glow, dryness, sensitivity.
- Skin type and sensitivity.
- Skin tone / Fitzpatrick self-assessment.
- Current products.
- Treatments tried before.
- Budget.
- Comfort level: at-home only, open to facials, open to lasers, open to injectables, open to consult.
- Downtime tolerance.
- Location.
- Timeline: browsing, this month, before an event, ongoing maintenance.

Output:

**Your Skin Plan**

- At-home routine opportunities.
- Product conflicts.
- Possible treatment categories to learn about.
- When to consider a provider.
- Recommended local providers when relevant.

### 2. Skin scan and photo baseline

Use existing AI skin analysis capabilities to capture:

- Acne/breakouts.
- Redness.
- Texture.
- Pigmentation.
- Fine lines.
- Pores.
- Dryness/oiliness signals.
- Under-eye concerns.

Important: frame this as **cosmetic guidance**, not diagnosis. The app should avoid saying “you have melasma” or “you have rosacea” unless reviewed by a licensed professional.

### 3. Routine and product shelf

Users scan or search products they own.

The app classifies each product:

- Cleanser.
- Moisturizer.
- Sunscreen.
- Retinoid.
- Exfoliant.
- Vitamin C.
- Acne active.
- Pigment active.
- Barrier support.
- Potential irritants.
- Duplicate products.
- Conflicting actives.

The app gives a **routine simplification score**:

“Your routine has 3 exfoliating steps and 2 retinoid-like products. This may increase irritation risk. Consider simplifying before adding a treatment.”

### 4. Trial tracking

This is the defensible piece.

Instead of instantly pushing a med spa lead, the app earns trust by tracking:

- What product/treatment was started.
- Frequency.
- Reaction.
- Photos.
- Breakouts.
- Redness.
- Irritation.
- Dryness.
- Pigmentation.
- Menstrual cycle, sleep, stress, travel, weather, diet tags if user opts in.

Over time, the app can say:

“Your texture improved over 6 weeks, but pigmentation has not changed much. You may be a candidate to learn about peels, microneedling, or laser consults.”

### 5. Treatment education

For each concern, the app explains treatment options in plain language.

Example: acne scars / texture

- At-home: retinoids, exfoliation, sunscreen, barrier support.
- Entry-level provider treatments: facials, chemical peels.
- Advanced: microneedling, RF microneedling, fractional laser.
- Things to ask provider.
- Typical downtime range.
- Risks.
- Skin tone considerations.
- Expected number of sessions.
- Price range, if available locally.

### 6. Provider match

When the user is ready, the app produces a short list of local providers.

Ranking should consider:

- Location.
- Provider specialties.
- Treatments offered.
- Relevant before/after examples.
- Skin tone experience.
- Credentials.
- Reviews.
- Price range.
- Availability.
- Consultation fee.
- Financing.
- User preferences.
- Provider response speed.
- Safety/vetting score.

Avoid ranking purely by who pays the most. Paid placement can exist, but it must be disclosed and separated from objective match quality.

### 7. Lead capture / booking

The app sends providers a structured, high-intent lead:

- User goal.
- Treatment interest.
- Skin concerns.
- Timeline.
- Budget range.
- Prior treatments.
- Sensitivity notes.
- Photos, only with explicit user consent.
- Product routine summary.
- Preferred appointment windows.
- Whether user wants virtual consult, in-person consult, or direct booking.

This is much more valuable than a generic form fill.

### 8. Post-consult and aftercare loop

After the user books or completes a treatment:

- Track provider visit.
- Log treatment.
- Store aftercare instructions.
- Reminders: avoid actives, sunscreen, hydration, follow-up.
- Photo progress.
- Rebook prompts.
- Review prompt.
- “Was this provider a good match?”

This closes the loop and improves matching.

# Lead monetization

There are several ways to monetize provider demand.

## 1. Pay-per-qualified-lead

Provider pays when a user submits a consultation request.

Lead tiers:

- Basic lead: contact info + treatment interest.
- Qualified lead: budget, timeline, treatment category, photos, intake.
- Premium lead: appointment requested or booked.

Example pricing:

- $20–$40 for esthetician/facial leads.
- $50–$100 for med spa treatment leads.
- $100–$250 for injectables, lasers, acne scar, pigmentation, RF microneedling, or surgical consult leads.

## 2. Booking commission

Take a percentage or fixed fee when the appointment is booked.

Example:

- $10–$25 for facial/esthetician booking.
- $25–$75 for med spa consult.
- 5–15% of deposit or first appointment value, depending on legality and commercial structure.

You need legal review here because fee-splitting, referral-fee, and medical advertising rules vary by state.

## 3. Provider subscription

Providers pay monthly for:

- Profile.
- Lead inbox.
- Analytics.
- Before/after gallery.
- Smart intake.
- AI consultation summaries.
- Campaign tools.
- Retargeting abandoned leads.
- Reputation tools.

Example tiers:

- Starter: $99/month.
- Growth: $299/month.
- Pro: $599/month.
- Enterprise / multi-location: custom.

## 4. Sponsored placement

Providers can pay to appear in sponsored slots, but this must be disclosed. The FTC’s endorsement guidance emphasizes disclosure of material connections, and that principle should apply to sponsored rankings, paid badges, and influencer/provider relationships. ([Federal Trade Commission][3])

## 5. Consumer premium

Optional, but I would not rely on it early.

Premium could include:

- Advanced progress tracking.
- Unlimited skin scans.
- Routine history.
- Product conflict engine.
- Treatment planning.
- Event-prep calendar.
- Provider concierge.

Example: $7.99–$14.99/month.

## Best revenue mix

Start with:

1. Free consumer app.
2. Provider subscription.
3. Pay-per-qualified-lead.
4. Booking fee later.
5. Consumer premium after retention proves itself.

# Trust and safety strategy

This is critical. The more you move from skincare into treatments, the more trust becomes the moat.

## Rules

1. Do not diagnose.
2. Do not claim guaranteed results.
3. Do not over-personalize medical recommendations without licensed review.
4. Distinguish cosmetic education from medical advice.
5. Use provider review for high-risk recommendations.
6. Build escalation language for red flags.

## Red flags that should trigger “see a licensed medical professional”

- Painful cystic acne.
- Rapidly worsening rash.
- Signs of infection.
- Severe allergic reaction.
- Open wounds.
- Burns.
- New or changing mole.
- Severe post-treatment reaction.
- Scarring risk.
- Pregnancy/breastfeeding treatment questions.
- Medication conflicts, especially isotretinoin, blood thinners, immunosuppressants.

## Regulatory/privacy baseline

A direct-to-consumer app may not automatically be subject to HIPAA unless operating on behalf of covered entities, but health and wellness apps still face privacy obligations. HHS has specific mobile health app developer resources, and the FTC finalized Health Breach Notification Rule changes clarifying coverage for health apps and similar technologies. ([HHS.gov][4])

The FDA’s general wellness guidance is also relevant: staying in low-risk wellness/cosmetic guidance and avoiding diagnosis/treatment claims helps reduce regulatory risk. ([U.S. Food and Drug Administration][5])

Practical requirements:

- Explicit consent before sharing photos or skin data with providers.
- Clear disclosure when providers pay.
- Separate “best match” from “sponsored.”
- Data deletion controls.
- Sensitive data minimization.
- No selling user health data to unrelated advertisers.
- Strong security for face photos and treatment history.
- Legal review for state-specific referral fee, medical advertising, and fee-splitting rules.
